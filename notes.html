Biographical Annotations

Frances Wills
Frances Wills was one of the first African American women to serve in WAVES (Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service) as a female officer in the women's reserve for the U.S. Navy. She was commissioned as an ensign naval officer and served from 1944-45 in Northampton, Massachusetts as well as in New York, New York. Prior to her service, Wills graduated from George Washington High School and Hunter College (Frances E. Wills Thorpe). Furthermore, she continued with her education at the University of Pittsburgh, where she pursued a master's degree in social work. Taking advantage of the new opportunities presented by the U.S. Navy, she signed up to join WAVES and trained at Smith College's Naval Reserve Midshipmen's School along with Harriet Pickens (Frances Wills, Naval Officer Born and Frances E. Wills Thorpe). Following her training at Smith College, she taught naval history to new recruits at the Hunter Naval Training Station (Frances E. Wills Thorpe).

Harriet Pickens
Harriet Pickens was one of the first African American women to serve in WAVES (Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service) as a female officer in the women's reserve for the U.S. Navy. She was commissioned as a lieutenant (junior grade) naval officer and served from 1944-45 (’We Made it, Friend’ The First African American Female Officers in the US Navy). Prior to serving in the U.S. Navy, Pickens graduated from Wadleigh High School and completed her undergraduate degree at Smith College. She continued her education at Columbia University, where she received her master's degree in political science (Harriet Pickens, Naval Officer Born). Pickens trained with Wills at Smith College's Naval Reserve Midshipmen's School. Following her training at Smith College, Pickens taught physical training to new recruits at the Hunter Naval Training Station (’We Made it, Friend’ The First African American Female Officers in the US Navy.)

Miscellaneous Annotations

Fantome
The SV Fantome was a steel-hulled sailing yacht built for Great Britain’s Duke of Westminster in 1927, and later acquired by beer baron Ernest Guinness. She sailed into Seattle in 1939 and became stranded by the outbreak of World War II. After the war, King County blocked her departure in an effort to recover property taxes from Guiness. The vessel ended up resting at anchor in Portage Bay for a total of nearly 14 years.

The Exclusion Order
This is a notice for the Civilian Exclusion Order Number 20. The Civilian Exclusion Order Number 20 was an explicit order instructing people of Japanese ancestry to register at a designated Civil Control Station which was located at the Japanese American Citizens’ League Auditorium in San Francisco, California. The registration of people of Japanese ancestry was in preparation to relocate this segment of American society to internment camps during World War II.

WAVES
The Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service (WAVES) was legislated in 1942 through Public Law 689. The legislation enabled women to serve in women's reserve for the U.S. Navy. Even though the legislation never explicitly barred African American women from enlisting, racial prejudice and discriminiation in the armed forces prevented African American women from enlisting in WAVES. The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) lobbied for integration. Prominent political figures, such as Eleanor Roosevelt, also supported the integration of People of Color in WAVES. Under Secretary of the Navy, William Knox, had opposed the activists' pleas. In 1944, Knox passed away and was no longer a hurdle to the activists' cause. Activists continued to pressure law makers, leaders in WAVES, as well as President Franklin D. Roosevelt. In October of 1944, Roosevelt authorized the full integration of American American women in WAVES. Harriet Pickens and Frances Wills were the first African American women to enlist in WAVES, complete the officer training program, and serve as naval officers (’We Made it, Friend’ The First African American Female Officers in the US Navy).

The March of Time
"The March of Time" Refers to a series of American news reels released in movie theatres between 1935 and 1951. They depicted many different topics. Many episodes were on the topic of war during WWII. Currently the News Reels are owned by HBO and can be viewed on Kanopy.
